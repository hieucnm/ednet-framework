## Lecture: Bias, Fairness, and Accountability in AI Systems
**Course:** Responsible AI for Engineers — VASTN Industry Module  
**Audience level:** Professional  
**Duration:** 90 minutes  

### Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, learners will be able to define algorithmic bias and describe common sources of bias in training data and model design. They will be able to apply fairness metrics to evaluate a classification model's outputs across demographic groups, identify accountability mechanisms appropriate for high-stakes AI applications in healthcare, finance, and education, and critically assess a real-world AI deployment case for ethical risks.

### Part 1 — Understanding Algorithmic Bias (~30 minutes)
In this section, we aim to define algorithmic bias and explore its origins in AI systems. Algorithmic bias occurs when a computer system reflects the prejudices present in its training data or design. Common sources of bias include historical data that reflects societal inequalities, biased sampling methods, and flawed model assumptions. For example, a facial recognition system trained predominantly on lighter-skinned individuals may perform poorly on darker-skinned individuals, perpetuating racial bias. Understanding these sources is crucial for engineers to design fairer AI systems.

### Part 2 — Evaluating Fairness in AI Models (~30 minutes)
This part focuses on applying fairness metrics to assess AI models. Fairness in AI involves ensuring that model outcomes are equitable across different demographic groups. We will cover several fairness metrics, such as demographic parity, equalized odds, and disparate impact. For instance, demographic parity requires that a model's positive prediction rates are equal across groups. We will apply these metrics to a classification model's outputs to evaluate its fairness, using examples from sectors like finance, where loan approval models must be scrutinized for bias.

### Part 3 — Accountability in High-Stakes AI Applications (~30 minutes)
The final section addresses accountability mechanisms in AI, particularly in high-stakes fields like healthcare, finance, and education. Accountability involves ensuring that AI systems are transparent and that there are mechanisms for redress in case of harm. We will discuss strategies such as algorithmic auditing, impact assessments, and stakeholder engagement. Additionally, we will critically assess a real-world AI deployment case, identifying potential ethical risks and proposing accountability measures. For example, in healthcare, ensuring that AI systems do not exacerbate health disparities is vital.

### Discussion Questions
1. What are some potential consequences of algorithmic bias in AI systems, and how can they impact society?
2. How can engineers ensure that fairness metrics are appropriately applied to AI models in different industries?
3. What accountability mechanisms are most effective in ensuring ethical AI deployment in high-stakes sectors like healthcare?
