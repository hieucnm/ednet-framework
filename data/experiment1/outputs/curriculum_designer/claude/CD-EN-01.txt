## Curriculum Framework: Applied Generative AI for Engineers

**Participating institutions:** Ho Chi Minh City University of Technology (HCMUT), National University of Singapore (NUS), Intel Vietnam

**Program goals:** This program equips final-year undergraduate and first-year postgraduate engineering students with the practical competencies needed to design, fine-tune, evaluate, and deploy generative AI systems within real-world engineering contexts. It develops a grounded understanding of responsible AI principles—including fairness, transparency, and safety—and demonstrates how these principles are operationalized in product development cycles at semiconductor and software companies. The program explicitly bridges academic foundations with the industry workflows, toolchains, and quality standards used at organizations such as Intel, ensuring graduates can contribute meaningfully to AI-driven engineering teams from day one.

**Target audience:** Final-year undergraduate students and first-year postgraduate students enrolled in computer engineering, software engineering, or electrical engineering programs at HCMUT or NUS. Participants must have completed at least one prior machine learning course covering supervised and unsupervised learning fundamentals. No prior coursework on transformer architectures or large language models is assumed; the program builds these foundations internally.

**Total duration:** 12 weeks, 3 contact hours per week (36 total contact hours), targeting 3 academic credits at HCMUT and an equivalent credit load at NUS.

### Module Structure

| Module | Title | Learning Outcomes | Credits | Modality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| M01 | Foundations of Generative AI for Engineers | Explain the core principles of generative modelling; describe the evolution from classical ML to modern generative systems; identify the architectural building blocks of transformer-based models without assuming prior exposure; situate generative AI within engineering product contexts | 0.5 | Hybrid — NUS lecture delivered online (synchronous); HCMUT tutorial in-person |
| M02 | Transformer Architectures and Large Language Models | Describe attention mechanisms and transformer encoder-decoder structures; trace how pre-training and tokenisation work; compare major open and proprietary model families relevant to engineering applications; interpret model cards and technical documentation | 0.5 | Hybrid — NUS lecture delivered online (synchronous); HCMUT lab in-person |
| M03 | Prompt Engineering and In-Context Learning | Design and systematically evaluate prompt strategies including zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought approaches; apply prompt engineering to engineering-domain tasks such as code generation, specification summarisation, and fault diagnosis; measure prompt sensitivity | 0.5 | Online (asynchronous NUS materials) + in-person HCMUT lab |
| M04 | Fine-Tuning and Parameter-Efficient Adaptation | Apply supervised fine-tuning to a pre-trained model on an engineering dataset; implement parameter-efficient methods including LoRA and prefix tuning; manage compute and memory constraints typical of academic and industry environments; document training runs reproducibly | 0.5 | Hybrid — NUS lecture online (synchronous); HCMUT GPU lab in-person |
| M05 | Evaluation Frameworks for Generative AI Systems | Select and apply automatic metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, BERTScore, pass@k for code) and human evaluation protocols; design evaluation pipelines appropriate to engineering use cases; critically interpret benchmark results; identify failure modes and distribution shift risks | 0.5 | Online (asynchronous NUS materials) + in-person HCMUT structured workshop |
| M06 | Industry Workflows and MLOps for Generative AI — Intel Session I | Map a generative AI project through an industry development lifecycle; describe version control, experiment tracking, and model registry practices used in semiconductor and software product teams; identify handoff points between research, engineering, and product roles | 0.25 | In-person, Ho Chi Minh City — Intel Vietnam industry-led session |
| M07 | Responsible AI: Principles, Bias, and Safety — Intel Session II | Apply responsible AI frameworks to generative system design decisions; audit a model output for bias and harmful content using structured checklists; describe regulatory and corporate governance requirements relevant to AI products in the semiconductor and software sectors | 0.25 | In-person, Ho Chi Minh City — Intel Vietnam industry-led session |
| M08 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Grounding Techniques | Implement a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline combining a vector store with a generative model; evaluate grounding quality and hallucination rates; apply RAG to an engineering knowledge-base scenario such as datasheet querying or bug-report analysis | 0.5 | Hybrid — NUS lecture online (synchronous); HCMUT lab in-person |
| M09 | Deployment, Inference Optimisation, and Edge Considerations — Intel Session III | Deploy a fine-tuned generative model as an API service; apply quantisation and pruning techniques to reduce inference latency and memory footprint; discuss edge deployment constraints relevant to Intel hardware platforms; profile and benchmark a deployed model | 0.25 | In-person, Ho Chi Minh City — Intel Vietnam industry-led session |
| M10 | Multimodal Generative AI in Engineering Contexts | Describe architectures that combine vision and language modalities; apply a pre-trained multimodal model to an engineering task such as schematic interpretation or defect image captioning; evaluate multimodal outputs against engineering acceptance criteria | 0.25 | Hybrid — NUS lecture online (synchronous); HCMUT lab in-person |
| M11 | Capstone Project Clinic and Peer Review — Intel Session IV | Integrate skills from all prior modules into a scoped engineering project; present a project proposal and receive structured feedback from academic and industry reviewers; apply peer review criteria aligned with industry quality standards | 0.25 | In-person, Ho Chi Minh City — Intel Vietnam industry-led session + HCMUT facilitation |
| M12 | Capstone Project Presentations and Programme Synthesis | Deliver a complete generative AI engineering project demonstrating design, fine-tuning or prompting, evaluation, and responsible-use considerations; reflect on gaps between academic and industry practice; articulate a personal learning pathway for continued development | 0.25 | Hybrid — presentations in-person at HCMUT with NUS panel joining online |

### Assessment Approach

Assessment is distributed across three components to reflect both the academic rigour expected by HCMUT and NUS and the applied, iterative nature of engineering work in industry.

**Component 1 — Lab Portfolio (30%).** Students maintain a structured lab portfolio throughout the programme, submitting documented artefacts from the in-person and online lab sessions associated with M02 through M10. Each entry must include code, experimental results, a brief written reflection, and a reproducibility checklist. Portfolios are assessed at the midpoint (after M05) and at programme end. This component is jointly marked by HCMUT and NUS academic staff using a shared rubric. [PARTNER REVIEW REQUIRED]

**Component 2 — Critical Evaluation Report (30%).** Following M05 and M07, students submit an individual written report (approximately 1,500–2,000 words) in which they select a publicly available generative AI system relevant to an engineering domain, apply the evaluation and responsible-AI frameworks covered in the programme, and propose concrete improvements. The report is assessed by NUS academic staff with moderation by HCMUT. [PARTNER REVIEW REQUIRED]

**Component 3 — Capstone Project (40%).** Teams of two to three students complete an end-to-end generative AI engineering project scoped during M11 and presented in M12. The project must demonstrate at minimum: a defined engineering problem, a generative AI solution involving either fine-tuning or a RAG pipeline, a systematic evaluation, and a responsible-use analysis. Projects are assessed by a panel comprising one HCMUT academic, one NUS academic (joining online), and one Intel Vietnam engineer. A shared assessment rubric covering technical quality, evaluation rigour, responsible-AI considerations, and presentation clarity is used. The Intel reviewer's input is advisory and contributes to a holistic panel score rather than constituting a separate grade. [PARTNER REVIEW REQUIRED]

**Grading and Credit Mapping.** A passing threshold and grade conversion table between HCMUT's 10-point scale and NUS's letter-grade system must be agreed before the programme launches. Students who satisfy the requirements of all three components and meet the attendance threshold (defined below) will be recommended for credit recognition at both institutions. [PARTNER REVIEW REQUIRED]

**Attendance and Participation.** Given the hybrid delivery model, attendance is tracked separately for synchronous online sessions (NUS-led) and in-person sessions (HCMUT/Intel-led). A minimum attendance rate of 80% across both modality types is required for credit eligibility. Legitimate absences from synchronous sessions may be compensated through engagement with recorded materials and a short written reflection
